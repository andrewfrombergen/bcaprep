There are two essays: a 400-word personal statement and a 100-word activities and accolades statement. The personal statement should emphasize your interest and preparation for your top-choice academy. It should not be a laundry list of accomplishments (leave those for the second 100-word essay) but rather be a coherent and genuine narrative specific to you.

One of your goals with the personal statement should be to help the admissions committee picture you as a BCA student. A good way to do this is to connect your interests and talents to the opportunities that BCA offers. This requires you to do some research: what electives are you excited about? What clubs or extracurriculars are you interested in? As examples, you could mention your interest in conducting agriculture research at the Environmental Science Center or your desire to take proof-based math classes that, at the high school level, are relatively unique to BCA.

A common misconception among applicants is that it is desirable to emphasize that you are competitive. This is wrong; you should not write that you are competitive in your personal statement. BCA is a collaborative place with a lot of group work, so it is much more important to show that you can get along and work with others.

You will need three recommendations: one from your 8th grade math teacher, one from your 7th or 8th grade English teacher, and one from your 7th or 8th grade science teacher.

After you have received confirmation from your teachers that they will write your recommendations, I suggest having a conversation with each of your recommenders. This conversation is an opportunity to tell your recommender more about yourself and your interests outside of class; this will help them write a better recommendation. When speaking with your recommender, you are also encouraged to recall anecdotes of you in their classroom and mention pieces of work you did in their class that you are proud of because the recommendation is more likely to be vivid and beneficial if it includes specific examples.

Remember to be polite and grateful. Your recommenders are volunteering their own time to help you get into BCA, and they are doing you a favor by writing your recommendation.

Congratulations on getting an interview! This means your application made it through the initial review and is being given serious consideration. If the format remains the same as that of previous years, you will be interviewed by two BCA faculty or guidance counselors involved in the admissions process at the same time.

My advice for the interview is simple: trust yourself. Be friendly, professional, and confident in answering the questions that are asked of you. Be punctual, and read your own personal statement a few times before interviewing. It is important to be familiar with your own application since you want to both be consistent with and provide more context for it in the interview.

Be sure that you are prepared to answer why you want to attend BCA instead of your local high school and why you chose the academy you chose. You can also practice interviewing with your friends and family.

Probably, but not necessarily. BCA is a phenomenal school, but it is not the right fit for everyone. There are both good and bad reasons to apply to BCA.

Good reasons:
  • You are excited about learning
  • You want more of a challenge than what you got at your local middle school
  • You would like to meet other motivated, academically strong students

Bad reasons:
  • Your parents want you to go
  • You wish to learn only what your chosen academy focuses on (BCA students in every academy take courses in a range of fields)

When you apply, you must choose one of seven academies as your top choice and also list a second choice. You will not be able to transfer academies after you get in, so it is important to consider the decision carefully. It is generally rare to be admitted into your second choice.

But while you should think carefully, you should not stress too much: choosing an academy is not choosing an academic or career path for the rest of your life. A large proportion of BCA students do not end up in the field that their academy prescribes, and it is completely normal for your interests to change. There are plenty of examples of AAST alumni who go into law, ABF alumni who become physicians, and AVPA alumni who end up studying computer science.

It is perfectly okay to choose your academy based on what your interests are now while acknowledging that your interests may change. Most courses and activities at BCA are open to students of all academies, so you will have many opportunities to explore disciplines beyond what your academy covers.

Applicants deciding between the four science-oriented academies (AAST, AEDT, AMST, ATCS): AAST has the most flexible, general curriculum and is good for students who are enthusiastic about STEM in general but may be less certain about a specific subfield or are open to many subfields. AEDT is similar to AAST; relative to AAST, AEDT is more focused on hands-on applications over theory. I recommend choosing AMST only if you have a serious interest in biology and are committed to exploring a career in medicine. Consider ATCS if you are very interested in computer science, but you can also take computer science in other academies.
