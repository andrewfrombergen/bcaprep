The entrance test is a crucial component of your application. Below, I suggest strategies and resources to help you make a study plan. I recommend that you use the text box provided to track your progress as you study. For example, you can make an ordered list of study tasks you plan to accomplish and cross them off as you finish them. You will be able to save what you wrote and return to it in future sessions.

The English test is a 45-minute exam requiring you to read a passage and respond to a specific prompt about the passage. You can use the standard essay formula you were taught in elementary and/or middle school: an introductory paragraph followed by 2-3 body paragraphs followed by a concluding paragraph. On this type of exam, good structure, clear reasoning, and appropriate use of evidence will be more important than coming up with novel, groundbreaking insights about the passage; do not spend too much time attempting to be unique or extraordinary in what you write.

Here is a list of resources you could use to study, with more important items listed first:
  1. Sample English test (pages 21-23) released by BCA
  2. NJSLA Literary Analysis Tasks for grades 6-11 (not the Narrative Writing Tasks)
  3. Engage deeply with your middle school English classes and take the writing assignments seriously
  4. Make a habit of reading The New York Times and/or The Washington Post (consider getting a subscription)

The math test is a 60-minute exam with 40 multiple choice questions, each with five answer choices. Note that the math that is tested on the exam is quite different from the math you learned in your middle school classes. You should not assume that you will do very well on the exam simply because you excel in your middle school math classes. While your middle school math classes will cover some of the content, there will be a greater focus on sound reasoning and clever problem-solving as opposed to applying formulas and regurgitating concepts. Therefore, it will be somewhat more similar in format to a middle school math competition.

    Here is a list of resources you could use to study, with more important items listed first:
  1. Sample math test (pages 1-6) and seven sets of practice problems (pages 7-20) released by BCA
  2. 22 sets (1999-2020) of past AMC 8 competition problems (difficult but important, do as many sets as you can)
  3. NJSLA grades 6-8 practice tests (easier and less important; start with these if AMC 8 is too difficult)
  4. Past problems from the middle school math competition hosted by BCA (very difficult, focus on grades 4-5)
  5. One free set of past MATHCOUNTS competitions (school, chapter, state: all rounds applicable)
  6. If interested, get involved in your school's math team! This is a good middle school competition math book.
